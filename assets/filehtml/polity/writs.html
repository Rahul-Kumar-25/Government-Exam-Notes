Writs

  • writ is a formal written order.
  • issued by Higher court
  • To enforce legal rights (or) to do or not to do something
  • Borrowed from English law (UK)
  • powers in hands of Supreme court (32) & High court (226)

How Supreme court writ differs from High court writ

Supreme Court High Court
when FR’s are violated FR’s + ordinary legal Rights
writ jurisdiction of supreme court is narrower in this respect. High court can issue against person, govt, authority located within its territorial jurisdiction
Issue against person or govt. throughout the territory of India. Territorial jurisdiction of supreme court is wider than High court in this respect. Article 226 is discretion and hence High court may refuse to exercise.
Article 32 in itself is a FR’s and hence the supreme court may not refuse to its writs jurisdiction. —

Types of Writs:

(1) Habeas corpus

  • In latin word “to Have the Body of”
  • issued by court.
  • To a person who has detain another person to produce the body of latter before it.
  • Bulwarks of individual liberty.
  • Against Both public Authority & private individuals

Not issued where:

  • where detention is lawful.
  • proceeding for contempt of legislature or a court.
  • Detention is by competent court.
  • Detention is outside the jurisdiction of the court.

(2) Mandamus

  • meaning “we command”.
  • issued by court.
  • To a public officials.
  • Asking Him/Her to perform His/Her official duty that He or She has failed or refused to perform.
  • Against any public body, cooperation, inferior court, tribunal or govt. for the same purpose.

Cannot issued:

  • Against private individuals or body
  • To enforce departmental instructions doesn’t statutory force
  • when the duty is discretionary or on not mandatory
  • contractual obligation (to enforce)
  • President or Governor
  • Against chief justice of High court acting in judicial capacity

(3) Prohibition

  • meaning “to forbid”
  • issued by Higher court to a lower court or Tribunal.
  • To prevent the latter from exceeding its jurisdiction.
  • issued only against Judicial authorities.
  • Not available against administrative authority, legislative bodies & private individuals or Bodies.

(4) Certiorari

  • Meaning “to be certified” or “to be informed”
  • issued by Higher court to lower court or Tribunal
  • To quash the orders.
  • issued on the grounds of excess of jurisdiction or lack of jurisdiction.
  • issued after the final order is passed.
  • only against judicial Authority.
  • Not available against administrative authority, legislative Bodies and private individual or bodies.

(5) Quo-warranto

  • “By what Authority” or “warrant”.
  • issued by court to enquire into legality of claim of a person to public office.
  • literal meaning – “Are you holding this position?”
