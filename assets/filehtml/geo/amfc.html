Air Mass, Fronts & Cyclones

Air Mass

  • Large body of air over the surface with no divergent winds.
  • Temperature, moisture content, lapse rate are more or less uniform horizontally for 100 km.
  • Cover large portion of continent and thick in vertical direction may extend to troposphere.
  • Air mass is cold when temperature is lower than the underlying surface, and warm when temperature is higher than underlying surface.
  • When two air masses of different temperature and humidity approach each other, fronts are formed.

Fronts

  • Three dimensional sloping boundary which separate two opposing air masses having contrasting characteristics.
  • Frontal Zone: extensive transitional zone between convergent air masses.
  • It represents zone of air discontinuity.

4 Types of Fronts

1. Warm Front

  • Warm air mass advances over cold one.
  • Gradual weather changes.
  • Steady precipitation and overcast skies.

2. Cold Front

  • Cold air mass displaces warm air mass forcing it to rise rapidly.
  • Intense but short time precipitation.
  • Thunderstorms.
  • Cumulonimbus cloud.
  • Snowfall.
  • Hailstorm.

3. Stationary Front

  • Boundary between two air masses where neither is moving or displacing other.
  • Prolonged overcast skies and intermittent precipitation.

4. Occluded Front

  • Cold front overtakes warm front lifting warm air completely off the ground.
  • Complex weather pattern with both cold and warm front features.

Cyclones

  • Areas of low pressure surrounded by isobars having high pressure.
  • Cyclones are low pressure system.
  • Wind moves from high pressure to low pressure with great velocity.
  • Isobars are placed closely (concentrated).

Also Known As

  • Hurricanes – Atlantic Ocean, North-East Pacific Ocean.
  • Tornadoes – Over land only (US & some parts of Europe).
  • Tropical Typhoon – Indian Ocean, North-West Pacific Ocean & South Pacific Ocean.
  • Willy-willies – Australia.
  • Baguios – Philippines.
  • Ciclones – Mexico.
  • Tainas – Haiti.

Movement of Cyclones

  • Northern Hemisphere – Counter clockwise wind movement.
  • Southern Hemisphere – Clockwise wind movement.

Eye of Cyclone

  • Centre of cyclone is also known as eye of cyclone.
  • 600–1200 km diameter of cyclone (India).
  • July–October (Maximum cyclones in India – Eastern Coast).

Conditions for Cyclone Formation

  • Cyclone requires 27°C temperature to form.
  • Cyclones form on water large bodies only.

Anticyclones

  • These are high pressure system.
  • Do not form clouds.
  • Have lower wind speed than cyclones.
  • Isobars are not placed closely.

Movement of Anticyclones

  • Northern Hemisphere – Clockwise wind direction.
  • Southern Hemisphere – Counter clockwise wind direction.
