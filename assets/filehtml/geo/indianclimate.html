Geography

Basic - Indian Climate

Indian Climate

  • Tropical monsoon type – During summer wind blows from South West to North East.
  • During winter wind blows from North East to South West.
  • Indian North is above Tropic of Cancer, it should have temperate climate, yet we have tropical climate because Himalayas prevent the cold dry winds from Central Asia.

Factors Affecting Indian Climate

  • Distance from sea
  • Himalayas
  • Location & latitude
  • Monsoon winds

Climatic Regions of India

  • Winter in India: Mid December to Mid March
  • Summer in India: Mid March to May
  • Rains in India: June to September
  • Retreating Monsoon: October to Mid December
  • Biodiversity rich in such tropical climatic condition

Note: Natural Vegetation (Refer to what’s given)

Monsoon in India

1. South West Monsoon (June to September)

  • This is our primary rain season
  • 70% to 90% annual rainfall
  • South East trade winds cross the equator and are deflected by Coriolis force becoming South West monsoon wind
  • Arrival is in Kerala in mid July
  • It has two branches
  • Arabian Sea branch hitting Western Ghats
  • Interior of Maharashtra remains dry because it falls in rain shadow area
  • Bay of Bengal branch hitting North East India and redirected westward by Himalayas

2. North East Monsoon (October to December)

  • It is also known as retreating monsoon
  • South East coast receives heavy rainfall
  • Sometimes cyclones also occur

El Nino & La Nina

El Nino

  • Unusual warming of surface water in the eastern tropical ocean
  • Amount of precipitation in coastal area of South America is high
  • Eastern Australia, Indonesia and South East Asia get drought condition
  • Poor monsoon over subcontinent

La Nina

  • Water temperature in eastern Pacific Ocean gets colder than usual
  • Strong high pressure over eastern equatorial Pacific Ocean
  • Drought in Peru and Ecuador
  • High monsoon in India (result of La Nina)

Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD)

  • Also known as Indian Nino
  • IOD + : When Western Indian Ocean becomes warmer than Eastern Indian Ocean
  • IOD − : When Eastern Indian Ocean becomes warmer than Western Indian Ocean

Forms of Condensation

  • Dew
  • Frost
  • Fog
  • Mist
  • Cloud

Dew

Water droplets on cooler surface of solid object which implies dew point above freezing point.

Frost

Condensation at a dew point which is below freezing point (ice crystals instead of water droplets).

Fog

Cloud with its base at or very near to the ground.

Mist

Tiny vapour that gets suspended in the air. (Mist contains more moisture than fog)

Cloud

Covered sky condition.

Forms of Precipitation

Rainfall, Snowfall, Sleet, Hailstorm
