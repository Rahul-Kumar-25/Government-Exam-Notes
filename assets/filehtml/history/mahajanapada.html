Ancient History

Maha-Janapadas

MAHA JANAPADA (600 BC – 325 BC) (Second urbanisation)

meaning → MAHA → Great

JANAPADA → Territory where a tribe (Jana) settled.

  • Maha Janapadas were large territorial states formed from smaller Janapadas

Historical background: 6th century BC

  • mentioned in buddhist text "Anguttara Nikaya"
  • This period marks rise of cities, iron tools, growth of trade and currency, decline of tribal system.

Types of political system:

Monarchy → power in hand of king

ex: Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa, Avanti etc.

Republic (Gana-Sangha)

  • power with assembly
  • elected king
  • ex: Vajji, Malla, Kuru, Panchala

Magadha (The most powerful Mahajanapada)

Reasons for Magadha rise:

  1. Strategic location
  2. fertile Gangetic plains
  3. iron mines (Rajgir area)
  4. strong rulers
  5. used elephants on a large scale in its war against its neighbours

Important Kings

1. Haryanka Dynasty (544 BC – 412 BC)

King → Bimbisara (Shrenika)

  • 544 BC to 492 BC
  • founder of Haryanka dynasty
  • Magadha came into prominence under his leadership
  • AKA Seniya, was the first king to have standing army
  • Conquered Anga by defeating its ruler Brahmadatta
  • He was contemporary of Buddha and Mahavira
  • He sent a royal physician Jivaka to Ujjain when Avanti king Pradyota was suffered from Jaundice

Strengthen his position by three matrimonial alliances:

  1. His first wife was from Kosala named Mahakosala (sister of Prasenjit) who brought in dowry "Kashi"
  2. His second wife was the Lichchhavi princess "Chellana" from Vaishali who gave birth to Ajatashatru
  3. His third wife was the daughter of the chief of the Madra clan (Punjab)
  • He build the city of Rajgriha and made it its capital

Ajatashatru (492 – 460 BC)

  • He killed his father and seized the throne
  • He followed a more aggressive policy & gained complete control over Kashi
  • In this way Anga & Vaishali (capital of Vajji) were added to Magadha, making it the most powerful territorial power in the Ganga valley
  • Buddha died during his reign
  • Arranged the first Buddhist council

Udayabhadra (Udayin) (460 BC – 444 BC)

  • founded the new capital at Pataliputra
  • probably was murdered at the instigation of Palaka the king of Avanti

2. Shishunaga Dynasty (412 BC – 345 BC)

Shishunaga:

  • He was earlier a viceroy / Amatya, high ranking official
  • shifted capital to Vaishali
  • the most imp achievement of his was to defeat Avanti and make it a part of Magadha

Kalasoka:

  • 2nd Buddhist council held at Vaishali
  • shifted capital back to Pataliputra from Vaishali

3. Nanda Dynasty (344 BC – 323 BC)

* first Non Kshatriya

Mahapadma Nanda:

  • Also called Ugrasena in Pali Text because of his large army
  • He belonged to low caste or atleast a non kshatriya caste
  • He was the son of king of the Shishunaga dynasty by a shudra woman and thus Nanda were considered adharmic
  • The first empire builder of India and claimed to be the ekarat (sole sovereign) who destroyed all other ruling princes
  • Added Kalinga to Magadha
  • Also acquired Kosala
  • made methodical collection of taxes by regularly appointing officials
  • Also build canals and did a lot of irrigation work

Dhanananda:

  • last imp king of Nanda dynasty
  • credited with a huge army, it is believed that Alexander attacked Punjab during his reign but his huge army checked him from advancing toward Magadha
  • He was arrogant and oppressive ruler who imposed heavy taxes
  • identified with Agrammes or Xandrames of Greek classical writers

Economy of Mahajanapada

  • Agriculture as main occupation
  • use of iron plough
  • coins called punch-marked coins
  • growth of trade & craft

Religion

  • Rise of Buddhism & Jainism
  • reaction against complex vedic rituals and brahmanical dominance
  • both Buddha & Mahavira were born during this period

Society

  • Varna system became rigid
  • slavery existed
  • women position declined compared to vedic age

Invasions in India during Mahajanapada period (600 BC – 323 BC)

  • ancient India did not face large scale of foreign invasions like later periods
  • north western India experienced foreign contacts and limited invasions mainly by Persians & Greeks

Persian Invasion (6th century BC)

  • During the reign of Darius I and Xerxes
  • Area conquered → north west India (Sindh, Kabul valley and Indus region)
  • This region became Persian provinces

Effects of Persian invasion:

  • introduction of coinage system
  • improved administration & taxation
  • influence on Mauryan administration
  • use of Kharosthi script
  • development of road network

Note: Persian rule did not extend to the Gangetic plains

Greek Invasion: Alexander the Great (326 BC)

  • Macedonian ruler Alexander
  • Indian ruler defeated → King Porus (battle of Hydaspes)
  • Areas affected → Punjab region, north western India
  • Alexander did not conquer Magadha due to:
    • fear of powerful army
    • soldiers refused to march further

Results of Alexander invasion:

  • opened cultural contact b/w India and Greece
  • improved trade link
  • weakening of north western states
