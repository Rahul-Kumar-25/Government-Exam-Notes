üëâ Vedic Literature (1500 BC ‚Äì 600 BC)

  • Rig Veda was composed while the Aryans were still in the Punjab.
  • Vedic literature comprises four literary productions:
    • 1. Samhitas / Vedas
    • 2. Brahmanas
    • 3. Aranyakas
    • 4. Upanishads
  • Vedic literature was handed down from generation to generation by word of mouth. These are called Shruti (to hear).
  • Vedas are called Apaurusheya (not created by men but God gifted) and Nitya (existing in all eternity).

1. Vedas

There are four Vedas:

a. Rig Veda, b. Yajur Veda, c. Sama Veda, d. Atharva Veda

The first three Vedas are jointly called Vedatrayi.

a. Rig Veda

  • Oldest text in the world.
  • Contains 1028 hymns and 10580 verses.
  • Divided into 10 Mandalas.
  • Mandala 2‚Äì7 are called Gotra or Vamsha Mandalas.
  • First and tenth mandalas were added later.
  • Contains Purushasukta explaining four varnas (Brahmana, Kshatriya, Vaishya, Shudra).
  • Recited by Hotri.

b. Yajur Veda

  • Deals with procedures for sacrifices.
  • Recited by Adhvaryu.
  • Divided into:
    • Krishna Yajur Veda (Black Yajur Veda)
    • Shukla Yajur Veda (White Yajur Veda / Vajasaneya)
  • Written in verse and prose.
  • Shukla Yajur Veda contains only mantras.

c. Sama Veda

  • Verses mostly taken from Rig Veda and arranged for singing.
  • Collection of 1810 melodies.
  • Contains famous Dhrupada Raga.
  • Contains 1549 verses.
  • Recited by Udgatri.

d. Atharva Veda

  • Collection of magic spells and charms.
  • Used to ward off evil spirits and diseases.
  • Considered non-Aryan work.
  • Not included in Vedas for a long time.

2. Brahmanas

  • Describe rules for sacrificial ceremonies.
  • Written in prose and ritualistic in nature.
  • Attached Brahmanas:
    • Rig Veda ‚Äì Aitareya, Kaushitiki
    • Yajur Veda ‚Äì Shatapatha (oldest & largest), Taittiriya
    • Sama Veda ‚Äì Panchavimsha, Shadvimsha, Chandogya, Jaiminiya
    • Atharva Veda ‚Äì Gopatha

3. Aranyakas

  • Called Forest Books.
  • Written for hermits and students living in forests.
  • Concluding part of Brahmanas.
  • Interpret rituals philosophically and oppose sacrifices.

4. Upanishads

  • Literal meaning: to sit near someone.
  • Deal with philosophy of life, universe, self and soul.
  • Total 108 Upanishads.
  • Focus on Atman and Brahman.
  • Known as Vedanta.
  • Mandukya Upanishad is the largest.
  • Brihadaranyaka Upanishad is the oldest.

1. Vedangas

  • Means limbs of Vedas.
  • Supplementary texts for understanding Vedas.
  • Human origin, not Shruti.
  • Written in Sutra form. Sutras are very short condensed statements that are used to express varied ideas.
  • Six Sutras:
    • Shiksha
    • Kalpa Sutras
    • Jyotisha
    • Vyakarana
    • Nirukta ‚Äì oldest dictionary
    • Chhanda
  • Shiksha ‚Äì Pratishakhya is oldest text
  • Vyakarana ‚Äì Ashtadhyayi is oldest
  • Chhanda ‚Äì Pingala text
  • Jyotisha ‚Äì Lagadha Muni
  • Kalpa Sutras:
    • Shrauta Sutras ‚Äì Vedic sacrifices
    • Grihya Sutras ‚Äì Family ceremonies
    • Dharma Sutras ‚Äì Varnas & Ashrams

2. Smritis ‚Äì Dharmashastra

Dharmashastra are Sanskrit texts dealing specifically with Dharma.

Dharma refers to the fulfillment of Purusharthas (life goals) such as:

  • Dharma
  • Artha
  • Kama
  • Moksha

There are 6 famous Smritis:

  1. Manu Smriti
  2. Yajnavalkya Smriti
  3. Narada Smriti
  4. Parashara Smriti
  5. Brihaspati Smriti
  6. Katyayana Smriti

Manu Smriti and Yajnavalkya Smriti belong to the Pre-Gupta Period.

Narada, Parashara, Brihaspati and Katyayana Smritis belong to the Gupta Period.

Note: It signifies ‚Äúa righteous moral law‚Äù which is in comsomance with universal natural law.

3. Mahakavyas

There are mainly two Mahakavyas:

Ramayana

  • Also known as Adi Kavya
  • Written by Valmiki
  • Oldest epic of the world

Mahabharata

  • Written by Ved Vyasa
  • Longest epic of the world
  • Contains 1 lakh shlokas
  • Divided into 18 Parvans
  • Harivamsa is its supplement
  • Bhagavad Gita is from Bhishma Parvan
  • Shanti Parvan is the longest Parvan

4. Puranas

  • The word Purana literally means old
  • They represent well-defined perceptions of past traditions
  • There are 18 famous Puranas
  • Matsya Purana is the oldest Puranic text

Important Puranas: Bhagavata, Vishnu, Vayu, Brahmanda

They describe 5 subjects across 4 Yugas:

  • Satya Yuga
  • Treta Yuga
  • Dvapara Yuga
  • Kali Yuga

Five Subjects of Puranas

  1. Sarga ‚Äì Creation of the world
  2. Pratisarga ‚Äì Recreation after dissolution
  3. Vamsha / Vansha ‚Äì Genealogies of gods and rishis
  4. Manvantara ‚Äì Periods of various Manus
  5. Vamsha Anucharita ‚Äì Royal genealogies (Shunga and Chandra dynasties)

5. Upvedas (Additional Vedas)

  • Rig Veda ‚Äì Ayurveda (medicine)
  • Sama Veda ‚Äì Gandharvaveda (music)
  • Yajur Veda ‚Äì Dhanurveda (archery)
  • Atharva Veda ‚Äì Shilpaveda (science of craft / wealth)

6. Shad Darshanas

Shad Darshanas represent the philosophy or way of seeing truth.

There are 9 schools of Indian philosophy called Nav-Darshanas:

  • 6 Astika
  • 3 Nastika

Six Astika Darshanas

  1. Sankhya Darshana ‚Äì Kapila
  2. Yoga Darshana ‚Äì Patanjali
  3. Nyaya Darshana ‚Äì Akshapada Gautama
  4. Vaisheshika Darshana ‚Äì Uluka Kanada
  5. Mimansa / Purva Mimansa ‚Äì Jaimini
  6. Vedanta / Uttara Mimansa ‚Äì Badarayana

Three Nastika Darshanas

  1. Lokayat (Materialism) ‚Äì Brihaspati (Real form: Charvaka)
  2. Jainism ‚Äì Mahavir
  3. Buddhism ‚Äì Mahatma Buddha

Early Vedic Period (1500 BC ‚Äì 1000 BC)

1. Core Region:

The early vedic civilisation was confined mainly to

  • sapt Sindhu

This Region included

  • Indus River
  • its 5 tributaries: Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Sutluj, Beas
  • Saraswathi River

Modern Area covered Punjab (Ind & Pak), Haryana, Rajasthan (North-west), Afghanistan

2. Imp. River mentioned:

  • Sindhu (Indus) ‚Üí most frequently mentioned river in Rig veda
  • Saraswati ‚Üí Described as a mighty River
  • Yamuna ‚Üí Eastern most River known
  • Ganga ‚Üí mentioned only once, showing limited eastward expansion

3. Extend of settlement:

  • North ‚Üí Himalayan foot hills
  • West ‚Üí Afg.
  • East ‚Üí Upto Yamuna
  • South ‚Üí Rajasthan desert Region

* The Ganga Plains were not yet fully settled during this period.

4. Nature of Geography:

  • fertile River valleys supported Pastoralism and early agriculture
  • Dense forest of eastern India were avoided
  • Rivers played a key role in settlement, economy and religious beliefs

5. Polity:

(a) Nature of the state:

  • No territorial state, polity was based on tribes (Jana), not land
  • The tribe was the main political unit
  • People identified themselves by tribal names, not by territory

(b) King (Rajan):

  • The chief of the tribe were called ‚ÄúRajan‚Äù
  • Not an absolute ruler
  • Selected from discussions and with democracy
  • Main duties ‚Üí Protection of tribe, leading wars, maintaining order, performing sacrifice

Note: King did not maintain standing army or bureaucracy

(c) Administrative structure:

  • Very simple and informal
  • No regular taxation
  • Chief dependent on voluntary gift (Bali)

(d) Popular Assemblies:

  • Sabha ‚Üí council of elders, judicial functions
  • Samiti (General Assembly) ‚Üí elected or approved king
  • Vidhata ‚Üí tribal assembly, social and religious matters

* Samiti was the most imp. and democratic body

(e) Officials:

  • Purohita ‚Üí Priest, advisor to king
  • Senani ‚Üí leader of tribal military
  • Gramini ‚Üí Head of village

(f) Law and justice:

  • Based on customs and traditions
  • No written laws
  • Assemblies played a role in disputes settlement

(g) Army:

  • No permanent Army
  • warriors were called ‚ÄúYodha‚Äù
  • used chariots, bows, spears

6. Social Structure:

(i) Tribal organisation:

  • Society was organised around tribes (Jana) rather than territory
  • The family (Kula) was the basic unit
  • Several families formed a ‚ÄúGrama‚Äù (village)
  • many villages together formed a ‚ÄúVis‚Äù and many Vis formed a Janas

* Kinship ties were very strong

(ii) Varna system:

  • Four varna existed but were flexible, not birth based
  • Varna was based mainly on occupation, not hereditary
Varna Role
Brahman Priest, perform rituals
Kshatriya warriors, rulers
Vaishya cultivators, traders, pastoralists
Shudra mentioned rarely, served other varnas

Note: No rigid caste system and no untouchability in this period.

(iii) Family system:

  • Patriarchal society
  • Father was the head of family (Grihapati)
  • Joint family system was common
  • monogamy was common, polygamy existed among chief

(iv) Position of women:

Women enjoyed a respectable and relatively free position:

  • allowed to attend assemblies
  • could perform religious rituals
  • had access to education
  • composed hymns ex: ghosha, apala etc.
  • marriage age was mature
  • practice of sati and child marriage didn‚Äôt exist
  • widow remarriage was permitted

(v) Education:

  • Education was oral
  • focus on memorisation of vedic hymns
  • pronunciation and recitation
  • education was available to both boys and girls

(vi) Food and dress:

Food:

  • milk, curd, butter, ghee
  • Barley (Yava) ‚Üí main cereal
  • meat was eaten

Dress:

  • simple clothing
  • Antariya (lower garment)
  • Uttariya (upper garment)
  • use of ornaments by both men & women

(vii) Occupation and social life:

  • main occupation pastoralism
  • cattle was a measure of wealth
  • no sharp division of labour
  • people led a simple, rural life

(viii) Slavery:

  • Dasa and Dasyus mentioned
  • slavery existed but was limited and mild
  • slaves could own property and sometimes gain freedom

7. Religion:

(i) Nature of Early Vedic Religion:

  • worship of natural forces personified as gods
  • Religion was optimistic and life affirming
  • No belief in moksha or renunciation
  • Focus on worldly desires like cattle, wealth, son, victory in war & long life etc.
  • Religion was closely connected with daily life

(ii) Major Gods:

  • Agni ‚Üí God of fire ‚Üí medium b/w gods and humans
    • present in domestic and sacrificial rituals
  • Indra ‚Üí God of rain, thunder and war
    • Destroyer of Vritra (symbol of Drought)
    • protector of Aryans
    • largest no. of hymns in Rig Veda
  • Varuna ‚Üí God of cosmic order
    • Upholder of Rita (cosmic law)
    • moral authority
    • controlled natural & social order

Other imp. Gods:

  • Soma (God of sacred drink)
  • Surya (Sun God)
  • Vayu (wind God)
  • Usha (Goddess of dawn)
  • Ashvins (twin Gods of healing)
  • Prajapati (creator)

* No dominance of Vishnu or Shiva in early vedic period.

(iii) Ritual and sacrifices:

  • simple yajnas performed in open air
  • no temples or idols
  • offerings included milk, ghee, grains, soma juice
  • Role of priests was limited compare to later vedic period

(iv) Life after death:

  • Belief in life after death
  • concept of heaven
  • no clear doctrine of rebirth or karma yet

(v) Religious text:

  • Rig Veda is the only source
  • written in Sanskrit
  • contain hymns praising Gods

8. Trade and commerce:

  • Trade was very limited
  • mainly barter system
  • No use of coins
  • Trade was local, not long distance

Later Vedic Period (1000 BC ‚Äì 600 BC)

1. Geographical Area:

(a) Shift from northwest to Ganga Plains:

  • Aryans moved eastward from the sapta sindhu region
  • The center of settlement shifted to the Ganga Yamuna Doab
  • This expansion was possible due to:
    • use of iron (krishna ayas)
    • clearing of dense forest
    • growth of agriculture

(b) Core Regions:

  • Ganga Yamuna Doab
    • became the heart land of later vedic culture
    • highly fertile alluvial soil
    • supported settled agriculture

(ii) Eastern expansion:

  • Upper Ganga valley
  • Kosala
  • Videha (North Bihar) * Eastern most area

(iii) Western & north western area:

  • Punjab & Haryana
  • its importance decline compare to the Ganga plains

(c) Important Rivers:

  • Ganga ‚Üí now the most imp. river
  • Yamuna
  • Saraswati ‚Üí declining in importance
  • Son
  • Ghaghara

* frequent mention of the Ganga shows its growing importance

(d) Janapadas (Territorial Units):

  • Society became territorial, not tribal
  • Land became the basis of political and economic life

* This laid the foundation for Mahajanapadas later

(e) Boundaries of later vedic Geography:

  • North ‚Üí Himalayan foot hills
  • West ‚Üí Punjab
  • East ‚Üí Videha
  • South ‚Üí Vindhya Region (limited expansion)

# Nature of settlements:

  • Permanent villages replaced temporary settlement
  • Growth of Agriculture & based rural economy
  • Beginning of surplus production

2. Polity:

(a) Nature of State:

  • emergence of permanent kingdom
  • foundation laid for mahajanapadas

(b) Kingship:

  • King continued to be called Rajan but
  • power became stronger and more absolute
  • Kingship became hereditary
  • King claimed divine sanction
  • New titles used like Samrat (Supreme Ruler), Adhiraja, Virat

(c) Expansion of Royal Power:

  • King was protector of territory, law giver, supreme military leader
  • maintain a standing army

(d) Role of Sacrifices:

  • King perform large sacrifices to legitimize authority
  • Sacrifices like:
    • Rajasuya (consecration of king)
    • Ashvamedha (claim of territorial sovereignty)
    • Vajapeya (symbol of supremacy)

These rituals strengthen kings political powers

(e) Decline of popular Assembly:

  • like Sabha and Samiti

(f) Administrative System:

  • More officials appeared like:
    • Purohit ‚Üí chief priest and advisor
    • Senanik ‚Üí Army chief
    • Gramini ‚Üí village head
    • Bhagdugha ‚Üí tax collector
  • Administration became organised and hierarchical

(g) Taxation and Revenue:

  • introduction of regular taxation
  • King collected:
    • Bhaga ‚Üí tax share of agriculture produce (usually 1/6th)
    • Bali ‚Üí compulsory tax not voluntary
  • Revenue supported Army, administration, royal rituals

(h) Law and Justice:

  • King became supreme judge
  • Brahmans gain influence in legal matters
  • Beginning Dharma as legal moral concept

(i) Army and warfare:

  • existence of standing army
  • use of infantry, cavalry, chariots
  • wars now fought for territory, revenue, political supremacy

3. Society:

(a) Social structure:

  • Rigid Varna system
  • The four varna system became rigid and hereditary
  • Birth, not occupation, determine one's varna
  • clear hierarchy developed like:
    • Brahmans (Highest Priest & teachers)
    • Kshatriya (rulers and warriors)
    • Vaishya (Agriculturist & traders)
    • Shudra (servants & labourers)

* Shudras were excluded from religious rituals & education

(ii) Emergence of caste distinctions:

  • beginning of Jati system
  • social mobility declined
  • concept of purity & pollution developed

(b) Family system:

  • Patriarchal joint family
  • father was the absolute head (Grihapati)
  • polygamy became more common among upper varnas
  • authority of elders increased

(c) Position of women:

  • Excluded from Sabha and Samiti
  • limited access to education
  • could not perform sacrifices independently
  • rising control male guardian
  • child marriage became began
  • widow remarriage discouraged
  • property rights were denied

(d) Education:

  • education became restricted to upper varna
  • mainly Brahmans & Kshatriya
  • Shudra were denied education
  • Religious text studied like Brahmanas, Aranyakas, Upnishads

(e) Ashrama System (Beginning stage):

  • Early form of Ashrama system emerged
  • Brahmacharya ‚Üí student life
  • Grihastha ‚Üí house holders
  • later two Ashramas i.e. Vanaprastha and Sannyasa developed gradually

(f) Food & dress:

Food:

  • Rice (Vrhi) became staple
  • milk products
  • meat consumption declined among upper varnas

Dress:

  • more elaborate clothing
  • use of ornaments increased

(g) Slavery and labour:

  • social exploitation became common
  • Sudras & slaves performed agricultural & domestic labour

(h) Social evils:

  • growing inequality
  • discrimination against lower varna
  • loss of earlier social freedom

4. Religion:

(a) Nature of later vedic Religion:

  • Religion became formal and ritual centric
  • dominance of priest

(b) Important Gods:

  • Decline of early vedic God like Indra, Agni, Varuna lost importance
  • Rise of new Gods like:
    • Prajapati (supreme creator)
    • Vishnu (preserver) (minor earlier)
    • Rudra (Shiva) (God of destruction and healing)

* This marks the beginning of later Hindu deities

(c) Sacrifices & Rituals:

  • Yajnas became long, complex & expensive
  • required many priest & offerings
  • major sacrifices like Ashvamedha, Rajasuya, and Vajapeya

(d) Role of Brahmans:

  • Became the highest & most powerful class
  • Controlled religious knowledge
  • Receive large gift i.e. Dakshina

(e) Concept of Karma & rebirth developed:

  • This idea justified the rigid varna system

(f) development of Dharma:

  • Dharma became central to religion
  • it meant moral duty, social obligation and varna based duties

(g) Philosophical development:

  • Reaction against ritualism led to Upnishad philosophy
  • focus shifted to knowledge, soul (Atma), supreme reality (Brahman)
  • This led the foundation of Indian philosophy

(h) Idol and Temple worship:

  • Still no fully developed temple worship
  • beginning of symbolic worship

5. Economy:

(a) Agrarian Economy:

  • Agriculture became the main occupation
  • extensive use of iron tools (krishna ayas) like ploughshares and axes for forest clearance
  • large tracts of Ganga Yamuna plains brought under cultivation

(b) Cropping & farming:

  • Rice (Vrhi) became the staple crop
  • other crops like wheat, barley, pulses etc.
  • knowledge of irrigation

(c) Land ownership & property:

  • Private ownership of land emerged
  • land became a source of wealth and political power
  • King claimed ownership over land and collected revenue

* This marks the rise of territorial state

(d) Cattle & pastoralism:

  • Cattle remained important but secondary to agriculture
  • Cattle used for ploughing, dairy products etc.

(e) Craft & industry:

  • Growth of specialised craft like pottery, blacksmithing, weaving etc.
  • clear division of labour

(f) Trade & commerce:

  • Trade expanded beyond local area
  • early use of metal objects like Nishka (gold ornaments) as medium of exchange
  • still largely based on barter system

(g) Urbanisation:

  • no full-fledged cities yet
  • growth of large villages and market centres (Nigama)

* These later developed into towns in the mahajanapada period

(h) Economic inequality:

  • surplus production led to accumulation of wealth by elites
  • poverty among lower varnas
  • economic inequality increased sharply
